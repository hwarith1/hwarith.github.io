Welcome

I study state-formation in post-colonial societies.

. In my ongoing and completed research, I show that states in the Middle East and North Africa supply high schools and mosques unevenly and strategically to facilitate social control, creating compliant and demobilized citizenries. These processes, prevalent in nations with historied of nondemocratic rule, leave behind manufactured meritocracies, territorialized marginalizations and enduring political fault lines.
By empirically investigating liminal moments of mass protest and transition in nondemocratic places, I seek to understand how individuals excluded from power can convert forms of associational and mass cooperation and cordination into observable political agency.
In my research more generally, I examine theories historically and spatially, leveraging a range of analytical tools, including quantitative and spatial econometric methods, to study educational supply, cleavage formation and mobilizational politics across those societies that decolonized after the Second World War. I have collaborated with colleagues to conduct empirical research on the macro-level drivers of identity construction in the developing world broadly.
Current and published research projects have so far explored macro-level processes in Tunisia, Egypt, Algeria, and Turkey. I have published co‑authored research with H.E. Ceyhun in in Mediterranean Politics, exploring the relationship between political polarization and religious identification in Turkey
In my dissertation, Fellahin into Frenchmen: Social Control and Educational Supply in the Postcolonial Maghreb, I demonstrate that regimes in Tunisia and Morocco withheld secondary education from regions linked to colonial‑era rebellions after independence.
I am grateful to be advised by Amaney Jamal, Mark Beissinger, and Elizabeth Nugent.
